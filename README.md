## Reflection

The client was DriverPass, a driver training business that wanted a system to help customers prepare for driver's license exams. The company needed a web-based system that would allow customers to register for accounts, access practice tests and training content, schedule driving lessons, and track their progress. The system also needed to support staff and administrators by allowing them to manage customer accounts, reservations, vehicles, and other system operations.

One part of my work that I did particularly well was organizing the system requirements clearly and connecting them to the needs of different users. In my business requirements document, I identified both functional requirements, such as scheduling lessons and accessing practice tests, and nonfunctional requirements, such as security, accessibility, and role-based access. In my system design document, I expanded those ideas into technical requirements involving hardware, software, infrastructure, and security controls. This helped create a design that was both user-focused and technically structured.

If I could revise one part of my work, I would improve the nonfunctional requirements by making them more measurable and specific. For example, instead of stating that the system should respond within acceptable timeframes or support secure authentication, I would define clearer standards such as target response times, password rules, account lockout thresholds, backup frequency, and uptime goals. Making these requirements more precise would strengthen the design because developers and stakeholders would have clearer expectations for performance, security, and reliability.

I interpreted the user's needs by focusing on the core problems DriverPass wanted to solve: helping customers prepare for DMV testing, simplifying lesson scheduling, and improving how staff manage customer and business information. I translated those needs into system features such as account access, online practice tests, scheduling tools, progress tracking, reporting, and role-based permissions. Considering the user's needs is important because a system can be technically correct but still fail if it does not solve the real problems of the people using it. Designing around the user helps ensure the system is practical, efficient, and aligned with business goals.

My approach to designing software begins with understanding the business problem, identifying the users, and separating functional requirements from nonfunctional requirements. After that, I organize the system into clear components and think about how users, staff, and administrators will interact with it. In the future, I would continue using strategies such as requirements analysis, UML diagrams, role-based design, and iterative refinement of system requirements. I would also place more emphasis on measurable system constraints, security planning, and traceability between user needs and technical design decisions so the final system is easier to validate and maintain.
